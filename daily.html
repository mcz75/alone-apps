Coach 10 min

Pattern to carry

What you wrote
Ich freue mich für heute
What it should be
Ich freue mich auf heute
sich freuen takes different prepositions depending on time direction.

freuen + auf (+ Akkusativ) = looking forward to something in the future
freuen + über (+ Akkusativ) = pleased about something that already happened

"Ich freue mich auf das CrossFit heute" → still in the future, use auf.
"Ich freue mich über mein CrossFit-Training heute" → it's done, you're happy about it, use über.
Memory hook: auf points forward like an arrow → anticipation. über looks back from above → reflection. The preposition tells you which direction you're facing.

The discipline of the identical meal

On decision fatigue, food monotony, and the quiet intelligence of habits

Barack Obama wore the same style of suit every day. Mark Zuckerberg rotates through identical grey t-shirts. Steve Jobs had his turtlenecks. The explanation they each gave was variations on the same idea: too many decisions are already waiting. Don't spend one on clothing.

This principle has a name in psychology — decision fatigue — and it describes something real. The self-control and judgment you bring to a problem at 9 am is not the same resource you bring at 4 pm. It depletes. Every choice you make, however trivial, draws slightly from the same reserve. The person who decides what to eat for breakfast, what to wear, which route to take, what music to listen to, and what to reply to their first email has spent something before their actual work has begun.

The identical meal is a small act of architectural intelligence. You're not eating rice and lentils and tofu because you lack imagination. You're eating them because the decision has already been made — it was made once, in a calm moment, with attention — and now it doesn't need to be made again. The cognitive overhead of feeding yourself has been compressed to near zero. You show up to CrossFit at 6 am with the same resources you would have had if you'd woken at noon.

There's a subtler benefit that gets less attention. The identical meal is also a sensory anchor. When you eat the same thing repeatedly, you stop deciding and start noticing. The lentils taste different depending on your state. You begin to read yourself through the food rather than through the food itself. Athletes use this deliberately. A recovery meal that's the same every time becomes a biofeedback tool: if it tastes flat, something is off. If it tastes good, you're ready.

The food photography project you started adds another layer. The camera doesn't lie about portion size, about the colour of vegetables slightly past their best, about the quantity of oil. What the photograph documents is not just what you ate but what you chose to show yourself about what you ate. Over weeks, the images become a different kind of self-portrait — one built from plates rather than faces, from choices rather than expressions.

The lean principle, as you put it this morning, is about not generating new chaos while sorting existing chaos. The identical meal is lean by design. It generates no new complexity, requires no new decision, and accumulates no new debt. In a morning already full of intention, that's not a small thing. That's the foundation that makes everything else possible.

Mini-lesson · German · Separable verbs
The 8 most useful separable verbs for daily life

Today you met vorbereiten. Separable verbs are common in German — the prefix detaches and moves to the end of the clause. Here are eight you'll use every week:

aufwachen — to wake up → Ich wache um 5 Uhr auf.
anfangen — to start → Wann fängst du an?
vorbereiten — to prepare → Ich bereite mich vor.
anrufen — to call (phone) → Ich rufe dich an.
abholen — to pick up → Ich hole dich ab.
aufhören — to stop/quit → Hör auf!
einkaufen — to go shopping → Ich kaufe heute ein.
mitbringen — to bring along → Was soll ich mitbringen?

The pattern is always the same: in a simple sentence, the prefix goes to the very end. In a subordinate clause (after weil, dass, wenn), the verb reunites with its prefix and goes to the end together: weil ich mich vorbereite.

Language · coming up
What the Einstufungstest will test

The Goethe Institut placement test typically covers reading comprehension, listening, and a short writing task. At A2/B1, the gap between levels is mostly about two things: grammatical range (can you use the past tense reliably? subordinate clauses? separable verbs?) and vocabulary breadth (do you have enough words to express nuance, or do you reach for approximations?).

Your spoken German is likely stronger than your written German at the moment — which is common. The diary practice is building the written side. By August, you'll have two months of German writing behind you. That matters. The test will place you correctly. Trust it rather than trying to perform above your level — being placed in a class slightly too easy is demoralising; being placed slightly too hard is exhausting. Accurate is what you want.

One thing to practise before the test: weil-clauses. "Ich lerne Deutsch, weil ich in Berlin wohne." The verb goes to the end. It sounds simple and it trips almost everyone at this level.
