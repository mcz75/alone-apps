Layer 3 · 10 min
Word
der Umweg
NOUN · masculine · /ˈʊmveːk/

A detour. The long way around. Literally Um (around) + Weg (way/path). It can describe a physical route or a figurative digression — a winding path to the same destination.

"Das war ein großer Umweg." — That was quite a detour.
"Wir sind über Szczecin gefahren — ein langer Umweg wegen der kaputten Oberleitung." — We travelled via Szczecin — a long detour because of the broken overhead wire.
"Manchmal führt der Umweg zum schöneren Ziel." — Sometimes the detour leads to the more beautiful destination.

Companion word: der Weg (way, path, route) — appears in dozens of German compounds: Ausweg (way out), Einweg (one-way), Wegweiser (signpost), unterwegs (on the way).

Layer 3 · 10 min
Coach — Today's Pattern

From this session: warten auf takes the accusative — always. This pattern came up in the train disruption and caught you out. Here's how to lock it in.

Wir warten auf die Entscheidung. (with "für")
Wir warten auf eine Entscheidung. ✓
Rule: warten governs auf + accusative — not für, not auf + dative. The article must also be present: eine Entscheidung, not Entscheidung alone.

Pattern: Ich warte auf den Bus · die E-Mail · das Ergebnis · eine Entscheidung.
Movement takes accusative. Waiting moves toward something — so accusative. Same logic as "in den Bus einsteigen."

Session also featured strong use of um...zu (purpose clause) with the comma placed correctly and zurück correctly positioned at the end of its clause. These are B2+ constructions used naturally — worth noting.

Layer 3 · 10 min
German Minute — Carry This Today
sich etwas gönnen
to treat yourself · to allow yourself something · to grant yourself a pleasure

One of the most distinctly German expressions — warm, guilt-free, deeply ordinary in native speech. You gönnen yourself a coffee, a slow Saturday morning, a plate of pierogi on a delayed train. It implies you've earned it, or simply that you deserve it because you're human.

"Gönn dir das." — Treat yourself to it. / You deserve it.
"Ich gönne mir heute einen freien Tag." — I'm allowing myself a day off today.
"Das habe ich mir verdient." — I earned this. (companion phrase)

The opposite: sich etwas versagen — to deny yourself something. Knowing both gives you the full register.

Today's Saturday in Poznań: Gönn dir's.

Layer 4 · 20 min
The Accidental Tour of Wielkopolska

There is a particular kind of Friday that arrives with a plan and departs without it. You board a train with a seat reservation, a novel, and the quiet expectation of arriving somewhere. Then the overhead wire at Opalenica snaps, and the plan dissolves into something more interesting: an improvised geography of western Poland, conducted at the pace of a replacement bus that hasn't arrived yet.

The Poznań–Berlin rail corridor, line number 3, is one of the oldest international rail routes in Central Europe. Built in the Prussian era, it connected the ports of the Oder basin to the industrial interior. Today it carries the Berlin-Warszawa-Express — sleek, fast, punctual in ordinary times — and also the ghost of every infrastructure decision made or deferred since 1945. The overhead wire, the Oberleitung, runs the full length. When it breaks, everything stops.

Świebodzin is a small town about halfway between Rzepin and Poznań. It is best known to tourists for its enormous statue of Christ the King, erected in 2010, which briefly held the world record for height. It is known to train passengers stranded by broken traction systems for something else entirely: for being the place where decisions are made, or rather, the place where you wait for them.

There is a German word for what happened next: Umweg. The long way around. The train reversed to Rzepin, then north toward Szczecin — a city you hadn't planned to see on a Friday evening in June. From Szczecin, east along the Baltic coast corridor, then south again to Poznań. A journey that should have taken ninety minutes took most of an evening. The WARS restaurant car, that peculiar Polish institution of hot food on moving rails, served schabowy and dill potatoes somewhere in Pomerania.

What is notable is not the disruption itself — infrastructure fails; the overhead wire at Opalenica has broken before and will again — but what the disruption makes possible. Attention, displaced from its intended object, falls on what is actually present: the passing landscape of Wielkopolska through a train window at dusk, the sound of Polish spoken on a platform in Rzepin, the specific weight of waiting for a decision that someone else will make.

The German you practised in those hours — warten auf, umsteigen, die Entscheidung — arrived in a context that will make it difficult to forget. Language learned from disruption tends to stick.

You used um...zu today without being prompted. This is a subordinating construction that most B1 learners avoid — it requires holding the main clause verb in place while inserting a purpose infinitive at the end. You placed the comma correctly. You placed zurück correctly.

The Goethe-Institut intensive starts in August. You have roughly six weeks. The gap between where you are and where you need to be for placement into a strong B1/B2 group is mostly consistency — not new grammar, but fluency with what you already know.
