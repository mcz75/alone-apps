Word · 10 min
nachdenken
German · separable verb · B1 · denkt nach / dachte nach / hat nachgedacht

To think something through — to pause and reflect rather than act immediately. The separable prefix nach- carries the sense of following up, going deeper. It is the verb you needed today: ich sollte nachdenken, bevor ich etwas liegen lasse.

Why separable?

In main clauses, nach- detaches and travels to the end: Ich denke kurz nach. In subordinate clauses, it stays attached: …weil ich nachdenke. In infinitive constructions: …um nachzudenken.

Ich denke kurz nach, bevor ich etwas kaufe.
Hast du darüber nachgedacht?
Er hat lange nachgedacht — und dann trotzdem das Falsche getan.
Memory hook Nach means "after" or "following." Nachdenken is thinking that follows — the pause after the impulse. Today's diary entry used it correctly: ich sollte nachdenken, bevor ich etwas liegen lasse. That sentence came from you.
Coach · 10 min · Pattern of the week

ist, not is.

This correction has appeared in every German session this week. It is not a knowledge gap — you know sein. It is a muscle memory gap: your fingers type is because English is faster.

Mein Blutdruck is 123/75
Mein Blutdruck ist 123/75
The verb sein in third-person singular is ist. You write this sentence every morning. It should be automatic by now — and it will be, once the habit is fixed.
Mein Ziel is
Mein Ziel ist
Same pattern. Mein X is is never correct in German. The rule is absolute: third-person singular of sein = ist, always.
Fix Before you finish any German sentence with a form of "to be" — pause. One extra letter: ist. Make it a physical habit, not a grammar rule.
German Minute · 10 min
bevor ich etwas liegen lasse
before I leave something lying around · from today's diary

This phrase came from you today — built correctly on the first attempt. Bevor introduces a subordinate clause, so the verb goes to the end: liegen lasse. The verb here is lassen used with an infinitive — liegen lassen = to leave something lying (to abandon it in place).

The pattern: lassen + infinitive

Ich lasse das Buch auf dem Tisch liegen.
Er hat die Tasche im Büro liegen lassen.
Lass das nicht einfach liegen — räum es weg.

Related: fallen lassen, stehen lassen

Fallen lassen — to drop something (literally or figuratively). Stehen lassen — to leave something standing, to abandon. Same construction, same logic.

Why it matters The habit you described in the diary — putting things down without thinking — has a precise German verb for it: liegen lassen. Language that fits the thought is language that sticks.

The Ramp and the Moat

On what green steel and a weekend apartment reset have in common.

Two words came into your vocabulary this week: ramp up and moat. They arrived from different directions — one from Steelwatch, one from a notepad at the desk — but they describe the same underlying structure. Every serious endeavour has a ramp and a moat. The ramp is the period of building. The moat is what remains once the building is done.

SSAB's Luleå mill is currently on the ramp. Prime yield in the first year of a new electric arc furnace operation runs at sixty to seventy-five percent — meaning a quarter of what the mill produces does not meet specification and must be recycled or sold at a discount. Energy costs are high because the scheduling system has not yet learned how to exploit the Nord Pool price curve. Automotive customers are withholding their qualification certificates because the process has not yet demonstrated sufficient consistency. The plant is losing money, and it knows it, and it continues anyway — because the ramp is the price of the moat.

The moat in this case is the HYBRIT supply chain: a dedicated flow of fossil-free direct reduced iron, produced with green hydrogen, arriving by rail from Gällivare. No other European steelmaker has this. The closest competitors are years behind. By the time they catch up — if they do — SSAB will have four or five years of operational data, mature automotive qualifications, and a cost curve that looks very different from today's. The ramp is painful. The moat is the point.

The wider the moat, the more painful the ramp required to dig it.

This is not specific to steelmaking. The apartment you are leaving in mild disorder before the weekend has its own version of this problem. The chaos did not arrive in a single event — it accumulated through a series of small decisions made without thought. Each object placed without intention is a tiny ramp in the wrong direction, a small widening of the disorder's moat. The fix you named — pause before setting something aside — is not a cleaning strategy. It is a ramp-up in the right direction. It will feel slow and deliberate for a while, and then it will become automatic, and then it will be the moat: a habit so embedded that disorder cannot easily get back in.

The ramp phase is characterised by effort that feels disproportionate to results. You correct the same German error three days in a row — ist, not is — and it still appears. That is normal. The ramp is long. But each correction is a deposit into a habit that will eventually become effortless. The moat of fluency is built one corrected sentence at a time.

Poznań this weekend is neither ramp nor moat — it is the thing both exist to protect. The people, the animals, the unhurried evenings. That is what the ramp is for.

📍 Steelwatch · Learning Feedback · Parts 1–8

You completed Parts 1–8 of the Luleå learning guide yesterday. Here is an honest assessment of what landed, what needs reinforcing, and what to watch for in Parts 9–12.

What is solid

The physical process. You have a clear mental model of the flow: DRI from Gällivare → EAF melt shop → QSP-DUE caster/roller → cold complex (pickling → PLTCM → CAL → CGL). You can describe each step and name the key equipment (Danieli Digimelter, SMS Group cold complex, PSImetals planning system).

The four constraints. Electricity price / zinc budget / DRI supply rhythm / night noise restriction — you absorbed these as a set and understand how they interact. This is the most important conceptual framework in the guide.

The liquid clock. The 30–45 minute tap-to-tap window is visceral for you now. That understanding will anchor everything in Parts 9–12.

What needs reinforcing

Grade distinction. STRENX (microalloying + TMCP, produced at Luleå) vs HARDOX (higher carbon + quench and temper, produced at Oxelösund) — the difference is clear, but the why of each grade's properties could be sharper. STRENX is structural steel for load-bearing applications; HARDOX is wear-resistant, used where abrasion is the primary force. Different customers, different supply chains.

The ramp-up yield curve. 60–75% prime yield in year one, 92–95% by year four or five. You know the numbers — do you know why yield improves? It is a combination of: chemistry calibration (the LIMS loop getting better data), operator skill accumulation, and automotive customers gradually granting qualifications that allow higher-specification production. The yield curve is a learning curve.

Svartbyn grid station. This is the critical path for full-capacity operation — delayed 12 months to 2029. It means the mill cannot run at full power until then. Keep this in mind when reading the economics in Part 11.

Watch for in Parts 9–12

Part 9 — Logistics: The Luleå→Borlänge slab shuttle disappears post-commissioning because the QSP-DUE produces finished coil directly — no intermediate slab transfer needed. Watch how the logistics map changes.

Part 11 — Economics: The green steel premium (how much more customers pay for fossil-free certification) is the number everything else is built around. Watch whether the guide gives a range, and whether it holds up against the grid delay.

Part 12 — Risks: DRI supply chain fragility is the one to watch. The HYBRIT plant in Gällivare is new technology. A six-month DRI supply interruption is not unthinkable, and the Luleå mill has no blast furnace to fall back on. How does the risk register treat this?

One question to carry into Parts 9–12

If the Svartbyn grid station is delayed a further 18 months — to 2030 or 2031 — what does that do to the ramp-up yield curve and the economics of the green premium?

Keep this question open as you read. Part 11 should answer it, at least partially.
