Layer 3 · 10 min
Word
Strohfeuer
NOUN (das) · /ˈʃtroːˌfɔʏɐ/ · B2–C1 register · lit. straw fire

A straw fire burns bright, burns fast, and is over before you've warmed your hands. Strohfeuer is the German word for an enthusiasm that flames up quickly and dies just as fast — the kind that produces three half-finished projects and an untouched gym membership.

It maps closely to the English phrase enthusiasm spike followed by abandonment (your vocabulary entry from 3 June), but where the English is analytical — describing the pattern from the outside — the German is vivid. You can see the straw fire. You can almost smell it.

„Das war nur ein Strohfeuer." — That was just a flash in the pan.
„Ich hoffe, dass meine Motivation kein Strohfeuer ist." — I hope my motivation isn't just a passing enthusiasm.
„Das alone-engine-Projekt darf kein Strohfeuer werden." — The alone-engine project must not become a passing flame.

The antidote to a Strohfeuer is not more enthusiasm — it is structure. Small, survivable habits that don't require energy to maintain. The alone-engine is itself a form of structure. Whether the project becomes a Strohfeuer depends on whether the system outlasts the excitement of building it.

Layer 3 · 10 min
Coach — Today's Patterns

Three patterns from this morning's session. All recurring — which makes them worth drilling.

Ania dived me to tram station
Ania drove me to the tram stop ✓
drove — past tense of drive. "Dived" = plunged into water.
tram stop — not station. Stops are for trams and buses; stations are for trains and metros.
the — countable noun needs the article.
I used other sit in a train that usual
I sat in a different seat on the train than usual ✓
seat (noun) not sit (verb). You sit in a seat.
on the train — you ride on trains, buses, planes. In a train sounds like you are inside the engine.
than usual — comparison uses than, not that. Recurring pattern: than/that confusion.
we were a little earlier that allowed me the quick switch
we arrived a little early, which allowed me to make the quick connection ✓
which — relative pronoun introducing a clause about the whole preceding situation.
connection not switch — railway term. A switch is a mechanical track device. You make a connection or transfer.
early not earlier — earlier implies a comparison; early means ahead of schedule.
On a train, in a car. On a platform, at a station. Than for comparisons, which for clauses.
Layer 3 · 10 min
German Minute — Travel Language
Ich habe den Anschluss noch erwischt.
I still made the connection. · from: Anschluss (connection) + erwischen (to catch, to get)

Den Anschluss erwischen is exactly the phrase for making a tight connection — catching the next train before it leaves. Erwischen has the sense of catching something just in time, with a hint of luck involved. It is colloquial and vivid.

„Ich musste über den Bahnsteig rennen, aber ich habe den Anschluss noch erwischt." — I had to run across the platform, but I still made the connection.
„Der Anschluss in Frankfurt — ich dachte, ich verpasse ihn." — The connection in Frankfurt — I thought I'd miss it.

Related: verpassen = to miss (a train, a connection). Ich habe den Zug verpasst. — I missed the train. umsteigen = to change trains, to transfer. Wo muss ich umsteigen? — Where do I need to change?

Ich fahre mit der Linie 14.
I take / I travel on line 14. · present tense, everyday usage
„Ich nehme normalerweise die Linie in der Nähe der Wohnung, aber heute bin ich mit der PESTKA-Linie gefahren." — I normally take the line near the flat, but today I took the PESTKA line.

Layer 4 · 20 min
The Straw Fire and the Engine

The word Strohfeuer entered your vocabulary system three weeks ago, in a session about the risk of starting the alone project intensely and then dropping it. Today, the risk re-emerged in a different form: not the project collapsing, but a single night of work burning bright enough to displace the morning CrossFit session. The fire is still burning. The question is whether it is a Strohfeuer or something more durable.

There is a useful distinction between enthusiasm for a project and commitment to a practice. Enthusiasm is the straw: it catches quickly, produces impressive heat, and is by nature temporary. Commitment is the log: it takes longer to catch, burns lower, and lasts through the night. The alone-engine sessions over the past two days had the character of straw — not as a criticism, but as a description. You were in flow. Flow is not the problem. The problem is what flow displaces when it runs past midnight.

CrossFit is not incidental to this. It is the closest thing in your current routine to a genuine practice — something you do regardless of whether you feel like it, something that has a physical cost and a physical reward, something that requires showing up when the conditions are not ideal. That is exactly the quality that the alone project is trying to build into its own structure. The irony of letting the project displace the practice it is modelling is worth sitting with.

The two issues logged this morning — #68 (Strohfeuer risk) and #69 (actor blind to vocabulary context) — are related at a deeper level than they appear. Both are failures of the system to hold its own knowledge across time. The risk was logged in vocabulary. The actor didn't check vocabulary. The pattern repeated. The system produced an outcome it had already diagnosed.

This is the tend problem at its core: a system that surfaces insights but does not make them available when they are most relevant has not fully solved the problem it set out to solve. The vocabulary entry for enthusiasm spike followed by abandonment was correct. The analysis was correct. The German word was apt. But none of that mattered in the session where it would have been useful, because the session did not know to look.

The fix is not more entries. It is better retrieval — which is a design problem, not a content problem.

Engine — Issue #69 in Context

Three angles on the actor-blind-to-context problem. Hold these while you work today — tend session when you're back.

The retrieval gap
The system knows things the actor doesn't.
Vocabulary, risks, coaching entries, and threads all contain knowledge about patterns. The selective loading map prioritises orientation files and task-relevant files — but it has no mechanism for surfacing pattern knowledge when a related topic arises mid-session. The actor's knowledge is bounded by what was loaded at session start.
What would a vocabulary-aware session orientation look like? A lightweight index (titles + one line) loaded at start — or a search step triggered when logging a new risk?
The tend design
tend should catch this — but it didn't.
The tend metatype is supposed to maintain the health of the system, including consistency and cross-referencing. But tend runs reactively — it fires when there is a session, not when a pattern repeats. The Strohfeuer risk appearing in vocabulary and then appearing again as a new issue three weeks later is exactly the kind of duplication tend should prevent. It did not.
Should tend include a periodic vocabulary review? Or a pre-issue-logging checklist that includes vocabulary search?
The lean principle
Generate no new chaos while sorting existing chaos.
The lean principle applies here: the fix to #69 should not require a new complex subsystem. The simplest intervention — a pre-logging check step in the tend workflow — is probably enough. A vocabulary index (titles + one-liners, maintained by tend) costs little and makes the knowledge available at session start without loading every file.
What is the minimum change to the tend workflow that would have prevented this? Start there.
