Layer 3 · 10 min
Word
to resolve
VERB · /rɪˈzɒlv/ · C1 register

To settle or find a solution to something — especially a problem, dispute, or open situation. More deliberate than solve (which implies a puzzle with one answer) and more final than sort out (which is informal). Resolve implies bringing something to a satisfying conclusion through effort or decision.

"In the evening I'll resolve some open threads in my work project." ✓
"The team resolved the conflict after a long discussion."
"The issue was resolved within 48 hours." — natural in professional writing

Compare: solve (a puzzle, a maths problem — one correct answer) · resolve (a conflict, a thread, a situation — requires judgement) · sort out (informal British; fine in speech, avoid in writing).

"*solve* some threads" ✗ — threads aren't puzzles
"*resolve* some threads" ✓ — threads require a decision
Layer 3 · 10 min
Coach — Today's Pattern

Two patterns from today worth carrying: platforms use on, and verbs vs nouns in compound forms.

in github
on GitHub ✓
Rule: Digital platforms, social networks, and services take on — not in.
On GitHub · on Slack · on LinkedIn · on Instagram · on Todoist

In = physical space or document: "in the file", "in the database", "in the repo". The platform itself is on.
setup app for Ania
set up an app for Ania ✓
Setup (one word) = noun: "the setup is complete", "a quick setup".
Set up (two words) = verb: "I need to set up an app", "she set it up".

Same pattern: login/log in · backup/back up · checkout/check out.
On a platform, in a file. You stand on a platform; you write in a file.
Layer 3 · 10 min
German Minute — Carry This Today
instanzunabhängig
instance-agnostic · from: Instanz (instance) + un- (not) + abhängig (dependent)

A compound adjective that describes the core design principle of the alone-engine: the engine must not depend on any particular instance. It holds the rules; the instances hold the data. Instanzunabhängig is exactly the kind of precise compound German builds naturally — where English needs a hyphenated phrase, German makes it one word.

*Die Engine muss instanzunabhängig sein.* — The engine must be instance-agnostic.
*Die Regeln sind instanzunabhängig; die Daten gehören zur Instanz.* — The rules are instance-agnostic; the data belongs to the instance.

German compound anatomy: Instanz (instance) + un- (negation prefix) + abhängig (dependent, from abhängen — to depend on). The prefix un- works exactly like English un-: unmöglich (impossible), unfair (unfair), unabhängig (independent).

Layer 4 · 20 min
The Engine and the Instance

There is a distinction in software architecture between the general and the particular — between a system that encodes rules and a system that holds data. The rules tell you how to behave; the data tells you who you are. A well-designed engine contains only the former. Everything particular — the specific vocabulary word learned on a Tuesday in Berlin, the diary entry written on a delayed train between Rzepin and Świebodzin — belongs to the instance.

This is the idea behind the alone-engine. The engine is not a diary system for Michał. It is a specification for how diary systems work. The difference sounds abstract until you try to build a second instance — a work version, say, or a version for someone else — and discover that the first version has accidentally encoded its own particular context into what should have been general rules. At that point, the engine is not an engine. It is a snapshot of one deployment, dressed up as a principle.

The nine metatypes — learn, practice, routine, reflect, track, act, surface, index, tend — were designed to be instance-agnostic. But instance-agnostic is a property that must be tested, not assumed. The test is simple: can you describe each metatype without using any noun that belongs to a specific instance? Learn is a behaviour, not a vocabulary file. Track is a pattern, not a KPI spreadsheet. Reflect is a mode, not a diary entry.

Today's use-case mapping is the first real test. You will take each metatype and ask: what does this look like in alone-personal? What does it look like in alone-work? Where do the answers differ? Where they differ, the instance is doing something the engine cannot prescribe. Where they are the same, the engine speaks.

The practical work of building Ania's app will sharpen this further. A second instance, even a simple one, immediately surfaces the assumptions you didn't know you'd made. She is not you. Her work is not your work. Whatever the engine gives her must be genuinely hers — not a copy of your structure with her name on it.

This is the work of the solstice Sunday: drawing a line between what is general and what is particular, in a project whose whole purpose is to hold the particular carefully.

Layer 4 · alone-engine
Engine Thinking — Three Questions for Today

Three questions to hold while doing the use-case mapping. Not to answer now — to let them do their work while you build.

Use-case boundary
What can the engine prescribe, and what must the instance decide?
The engine can say "a learn entry has a topic, a trigger, and a memory hook." It cannot say "the topic is German vocabulary." The instance decides what is learned. The engine decides how learning is structured. Draw that line for each of the nine metatypes today.
For each metatype: can you describe it fully without naming a single piece of instance-specific content?
Work instance
What does alone-work need that alone-personal doesn't — and vice versa?
alone-personal has language coaching, food logging, Steelwatch, CrossFit KPIs. alone-work has Gemini, work threads, professional register. The tend metatype — which shapes the engine itself — lives only in the engine repo. Both instances flag gaps upward. The work instance probably needs a lighter reflect structure and a heavier act structure. Check that assumption today.
Which metatypes feel different across personal/work — and is that difference instance data, or does it reveal an engine gap?
Ania's app
What does she actually need — not what you have?
The temptation is to give Ania a copy of alone-personal with her name in it. The better question is: what problem is she trying to solve? If the engine is well-designed, her instance should emerge from her use cases, not from yours. Scope before building. A ten-minute conversation about what she wants will save three hours of rework.
Before creating any files: what are Ania's three core use cases?
