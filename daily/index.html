Word · 10 min
Ballaststoffe
German · noun, plural · B1 · die Ballaststoffe / der Ballaststoff

Dietary fibre — the indigestible parts of plant food that slow digestion, feed gut bacteria, and keep blood sugar stable. Found on every German nutrition label, between Kohlenhydrate and Eiweiß.

The literal meaning

Ballast (dead weight) + Stoffe (substances). Ballast substances — weight that slows things down. The metaphor is built directly into the word: fibre slows absorption the way ballast slows a ship.

On the nutrition label

Under Nährwerte, you will always find: Kohlenhydrate / davon Zucker / Ballaststoffe / Eiweiß / Fett. The rice, lentils, and frozen vegetables you eat daily are high in Ballaststoffe — which is partly why the meal keeps you going through a 6am CrossFit session.

Roggen enthält viele Ballaststoffe — und das schmeckt man.
Wie viel Gramm Ballaststoffe hat das pro Portion?
Ballaststoffe sind wichtig für die Verdauung.
Memory hook A ship takes on ballast to stay stable and move slowly, controlled. Fibre does the same in your gut — stabilises blood sugar, slows absorption. You eat the same meal every day precisely for this reason: predictability is its own form of stability.
Coach · 10 min · From today's session

One pattern to carry.

You made 19 corrections today. One of them runs through almost all of them: the separable verb.

noch nich forbereite
noch nicht vorbereitet
Three things happened here: nicht lost its final t; the prefix was spelled for- instead of vor-; and vorbereitet needs the past participle form. The verb is vorbereiten — separable, with the prefix always going to the end in main clauses.
forbereite is fur
bereite es für … vor
vor is the prefix. It detaches and goes to the end of the clause. bereite … vor — the gap between them can be large, and the verb still holds together. This is the core separable verb pattern.

The rule to write on your hand

Ich bereite das Essen für CrossFit vor.
→ The prefix always travels to the end.

Other separable verbs you use regularly: aufstehen, anfangen, einkaufen, vorstellen, zurückkommen. Same rule, every time.

Read · 20 min

The Liquid Clock

On why steel cannot wait — and what that teaches about time-critical systems.

There is a moment in steelmaking when time stops being abstract. The furnace tips, two hundred tonnes of liquid steel pour into a ladle, and from that point forward there is a countdown that cannot be paused, reversed, or negotiated. The steel is losing roughly one degree Celsius per minute. You have thirty to forty-five minutes to get it from the ladle into the caster. If anything in that window fails — a crane, a chemistry reading, a blocked nozzle — the heat is lost. Two hundred tonnes of steel, alloys, and electricity, gone.

Engineers call it the liquid clock. The entire plant is organised around it.

What makes the SSAB Luleå mill so interesting from a systems perspective is that this ancient constraint — liquid steel that won't wait — is now wrapped in a web of entirely new constraints that are specific to this plant, this decade, and this location in northern Sweden. The electricity price on Nord Pool varies by the hour, and the electric arc furnace draws enough power to shift that calculation meaningfully. Cheap electricity mostly comes at night. But from month forty-nine of operations onward, a noise permit restricts certain outdoor activities after ten in the evening. Cheap power and quiet nights pull in opposite directions.

Then there is the zinc budget. The continuous galvanising line coats steel strip by passing it through a bath of molten zinc at 460°C. Air knives control the coating thickness. Zinc evaporates. The environmental permit allows 2,500 kilograms of zinc emissions to atmosphere per year — the regulator halved the original application. The scheduler must track cumulative zinc consumption in real time, the way a diver tracks remaining air. When the budget runs low, galvanising stops, regardless of what the order book says.

A blast furnace cannot be timed. An electric arc furnace can. That difference is where the whole scheduling advantage lives.

And yet: more flexibility does not automatically mean better outcomes. The SSAB planning system, built on PSImetals, must simultaneously honour the liquid clock, the electricity price curve, the zinc budget, the DRI supply rhythm arriving from Gällivare 120 kilometres north, and the night noise restriction. Seven software modules share a single data model. Human planners add the layer the algorithm cannot model: a key automotive customer threatening to switch, a DRI ship delayed by ice on the Gulf of Bothnia, a grade qualification test due before the quarter closes.

The lesson is not that complexity is bad. The lesson is that every constraint is a choice someone made — or a law of physics someone discovered — and the system's job is to honour all of them simultaneously without pretending any of them doesn't exist.

The liquid clock was always there. The zinc budget was added by a regulator in 2024. The electricity price curve is an economic signal from the Nordic grid. The night noise limit is a promise made to the residents of Luleå. All four are real. None cancels the others.

There is something clarifying about working with materials that have no patience. Steel at 1,550°C does not care about your planning horizon or your software architecture. It cools. You either move fast enough or you don't.

Parts 9 through 12 — logistics, labour, economics, and risk — come next. They will build the picture outward from the plant floor to the market, to the workforce, to the strategic bet SSAB is making on the future of green steel. But the foundation is always the liquid clock. Everything else is organised around the fact that the steel will not wait.

Steelwatch: Run 1 — Parts 1–8

📍 Steelwatch · SSAB Luleå · Learning Guide

Yesterday you completed the first eight parts of the Luleå learning guide — a 667-line overview of the full steel production process from iron ore in the ground to certified coil in the shipping hall. Here is the map of what you now know.

Part 1
Raw Materials
HYBRIT / DRI / HBI — iron ore reduced with hydrogen in Gällivare; sponge iron by rail to Luleå. Scrap as complement. Zinc from galvanised scrap enters EAF dust, not air.
Part 2
Electricity & Energy
2.7–3.4 TWh/yr for the mill; 400–600 MW peak. SE1 price zone = 30–50% cheaper than SE3. Svartbyn grid station = critical path, delayed 12 months to 2029. Q-One power feeder smooths EAF load.
Part 3
Melt Shop
Two Danieli Digimelter EAFs. ECS continuous charging. The heel — 20–30t left to keep the arc alive. LIMS chemistry loop: sample → spectrometer → alloy correction in 3–5 min. 33–45 min tap-to-tap.
Part 4
Casting & Hot Rolling
QSP-DUE: caster + rolling mill direct-coupled. No reheating furnace. Slab exits at 900–1000°C, induction heater tops up, straight into the mill. TMCP for STRENX. End of Danieli scope.
Part 5
Cold Complex
SMS group: pickling → PLTCM cold rolling → CAL annealing → CGL galvanising. Zinc-to-air cap: 2,500 kg/yr. That cap is the CGL scheduling constraint.
Part 6
Grades & Campaigns
STRENX: microalloying + TMCP. HARDOX: higher carbon + Q&T at Oxelösund. Four Luleå constraints: electricity price / zinc budget / DRI supply / night noise.
Part 7
Planning & Scheduling
Three horizons: 4–12 weeks / 1–7 days / real-time. PSImetals: 7 modules, single data model. Nord Pool ingested as scheduling input. Human planners add tacit knowledge the algorithm cannot model.
Part 8
Quality & Certification
EN 10204 3.1 standard. Automotive qualification: 18–36 months, plant-specific. Ramp-up yield curve: 60–75% → 92–95% over 4–5 years. Fossil-free cert needs separate documentation chain.

One concept to sit with

The liquid clock: from the moment the EAF taps, the steel loses ~1°C per minute. The entire plant is organised around a 30–45 minute window that cannot be paused. Every investment in the melt shop — the ECS system, the LIMS loop, the ladle furnace — exists to protect that window. Most planning systems treat time as a resource to be allocated. In steelmaking, time is a constraint that the material enforces without negotiation.
