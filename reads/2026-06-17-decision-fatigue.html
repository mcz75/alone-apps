The discipline of the identical meal

On decision fatigue, food monotony, and the quiet intelligence of habits
Wednesday, 17 June 2026 · Berlin · 6 min read

Barack Obama wore the same style of suit every day. Mark Zuckerberg rotates through identical grey t-shirts. Steve Jobs had his turtlenecks. The explanation they each gave was variations on the same idea: too many decisions are already waiting. Don't spend one on clothing.

This principle has a name in psychology — decision fatigue — and it describes something real. The self-control and judgment you bring to a problem at 9 am is not the same resource you bring at 4 pm. It depletes. Every choice you make, however trivial, draws slightly from the same reserve. The person who decides what to eat for breakfast, what to wear, which route to take, what music to listen to, and what to reply to their first email has spent something before their actual work has begun.

The identical meal is a small act of architectural intelligence. You're not eating rice and lentils and tofu because you lack imagination. You're eating them because the decision has already been made — it was made once, in a calm moment, with attention — and now it doesn't need to be made again. The cognitive overhead of feeding yourself has been compressed to near zero. You show up to CrossFit at 6 am with the same resources you would have had if you'd woken at noon.

There's a subtler benefit that gets less attention. The identical meal is also a sensory anchor. When you eat the same thing repeatedly, you stop deciding and start noticing. The lentils taste different depending on your state. You begin to read yourself through the food rather than through the food itself. Athletes use this deliberately. A recovery meal that's the same every time becomes a biofeedback tool: if it tastes flat, something is off. If it tastes good, you're ready.

The food photography project adds another layer. The camera doesn't lie about portion size, about the colour of vegetables slightly past their best, about the quantity of oil. What the photograph documents is not just what you ate but what you chose to show yourself about what you ate. Over weeks, the images become a different kind of self-portrait — one built from plates rather than faces, from choices rather than expressions.

The lean principle — refusing to generate new chaos while sorting existing chaos — extends further than meals. It's a stance toward all accumulation: nothing open without a reason, nothing held without intent. The identical meal is lean by design. It generates no new complexity, requires no new decision, and accumulates no new debt. In a morning already full of intention, that's not a small thing. That's the foundation that makes everything else possible.

Wittgenstein, whose Philosophical Investigations you encountered this morning at the Grenzgänger changing room, had something obliquely relevant to say about habits. He was suspicious of the idea that meaning lives in some inner mental act — a private intention, a feeling of understanding. What gives a word its meaning, he argued, is the practice surrounding it. Meaning is use. By extension: identity is habit. You are not the person who intends to eat well. You are the person who, in fact, eats the same nourishing bowl every day. The intention without the practice is noise. The practice without grand intention is enough.
